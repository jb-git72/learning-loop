The label says "natural." The supply chain says 2,400km.

You read the packaging. You check for "free range." You look for the green tick. But none of it tells you which farm grew it. What practices they used. How far it travelled to reach you.

That gap between what you see and what you know? It's not an accident. It's a business model.

FarmThru is a farm-direct grocery store built on one rule: if we can't show you exactly where it came from, we don't sell it.

Here's what a typical supermarket chicken journey looks like. Raised on a contract farm you'll never identify. Processed at a facility hundreds of kilometres away. Trucked to a distribution centre. Trucked again to a store.

By the time it reaches the shelf, it's travelled further than most holidays. The label says "farm fresh." The system says otherwise.

FarmThru works with around 15 to 20 partner farms and suppliers across NSW. Every product traces back to a named source. Not a brand. Not a distributor. The actual farm, the actual practice, the actual distance.

Transparency isn't a marketing word here. It's the entire model.

We'll show you where your food comes from. That's not a tagline we put on a tote bag. It's how the business operates.

You order online. You collect from our Brookvale hub, Monday to Friday. No unnecessary middlemen between the farm and your hands.

No subscription. No lock-in. No membership fees. Order when you want. Our bulk buying model means you save by buying more of what you already need.

Every step is shorter. Every source is visible. That's what food safety looks like when the system isn't hiding behind packaging.

Rachel Ward kept hearing the same thing.

"You can't give customers that level of detail."

From distributors. From retailers. From people who built careers inside a supply chain designed to obscure, not reveal.

She didn't argue with them. She built something else. A farm-direct grocery store where provenance isn't a premium feature. It's the baseline.

FarmThru exists because one person decided that knowing what you're eating shouldn't require a journalism degree. It should require a grocery store that actually tells you.
